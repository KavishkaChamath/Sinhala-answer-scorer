## Anuradhapura Kingdom - Ancient Sri Lanka History

### Administration and Governance
The Anuradhapura Kingdom (377 BCE – 1017 CE) was the first established kingdom of ancient Sri Lanka. It was founded by King Pandukabhaya in 377 BCE, who organized the city with distinct quarters for various craftsmen, healers, and citizens. The kingdom had a highly organized administrative system with the king at the apex. Ministers (Amatyas) assisted the king. Provincial governors managed regions. The capital Anuradhapura was a planned city with dedicated zones. King Devanampiya Tissa (247–207 BCE) was the first king to embrace Buddhism and had strong ties with Emperor Ashoka of India.

King Dutugamunu (161–137 BCE) unified the island after defeating King Elara, establishing Sinhala Buddhist identity. He built the Ruwanwelisaya stupa and the Lohaprasada (Brazen Palace). King Valagamba (89–77 BCE) faced invasions and had to temporarily flee the kingdom. King Mahasena (274–301 CE) constructed many irrigation tanks and was initially hostile to Buddhism before reconciling.

### Irrigation and Civilization
The Anuradhapura kings built one of the most advanced hydraulic civilizations in the ancient world. Major irrigation works included: Parakrama Samudra (though mainly Polonnaruwa), Minneriya Tank built by King Mahasena, Kantalai Tank, Nachchaduwa Tank, Tissa Wewa built by King Devanampiya Tissa, Abhaya Wewa (Basawakkulama) built by King Pandukabhaya.

The irrigation network consisted of interconnected tanks (wewas), canals (ela), sluices (bisokotuwa), and distribution channels. Rice cultivation was the primary agricultural activity. The hydraulic civilization supported a large population and urban centers. Engineers developed sophisticated sluice technology (Bisokotuwa) unique to Sri Lanka. Water management was considered a royal duty and religious merit-making activity.

### Buddhism and Culture
Buddhism arrived in Sri Lanka in 247 BCE when Mahinda Thero, son of Emperor Ashoka, converted King Devanampiya Tissa. The Bodhi tree (Sri Maha Bodhi) was brought by Theri Sanghamitta. The Thuparamaya was the first stupa built in Sri Lanka. Major religious monuments included: Ruwanwelisaya (Mahathupa) - built by King Dutugamunu, Jetavanaramaya - one of the tallest ancient structures in the world, Abhayagiri Dagoba - built by King Valagamba, Mirisawetiya Stupa - built by King Dutugamunu.

Pali Canon was written down in the First Century BCE at Aluvihara. Theravada Buddhism was the state religion. Monasteries (Viharas) were centers of learning and culture. Notable monks included Buddhaghosa who wrote Visuddhimagga. Buddhist art, sculpture, and architecture flourished. The Aukana Buddha statue is a notable example of sculptural art. Sinhalese literature and the Mahavamsa chronicle were products of this period.

### Notable Rulers and Events
1. King Vijaya (543 BCE) - legendary founder of Sinhalese people, arrived from India
2. King Pandukabhaya (377–307 BCE) - first recognized king of Anuradhapura, organized the capital
3. King Devanampiya Tissa (247–207 BCE) - converted to Buddhism, friendship with Ashoka
4. King Dutugamunu (161–137 BCE) - unified Sri Lanka, great builder of religious monuments
5. King Elara - Tamil king who ruled justly for 44 years before being defeated by Dutugamunu
6. King Valagamba (Vattagamani Abhaya) (89–77 BCE) - drove out South Indian invaders, Pali canon written during his reign
7. King Mahasena (274–301 CE) - great irrigation builder, Jetavanaramaya
8. King Dhatusena (455–473 CE) - built Kalawewa tank, father of Kashyapa
9. King Kashyapa (473–495 CE) - built Sigiriya fortress palace
10. Last phase kings faced Chola invasions leading to fall of Anuradhapura in 1017 CE

### Social Structure
Society was organized in a hierarchical manner. Brahmins, warriors (Khatriyas), merchants, and farmers formed the main castes. Craft guilds existed. Women had certain rights including property ownership. Slaves existed but had legal protections. Foreign merchants and traders were present.

### Trade and Economy
Sri Lanka was an important trade hub on the Indian Ocean trade routes. Exports included spices, gems, elephants, ivory, and textiles. Imports included horses, luxury goods, and metals. Arab, Roman, Greek, and Indian merchants traded with Sri Lanka. Coins were used as currency.
